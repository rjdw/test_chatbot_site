Petrarchan Love and Eve's Dream of Satan in Paradise Lost

The crappy college essay that got me wrongly accused of plagarism and caused a whole debacle.

In Book V of Paradise Lost, Milton uses Eve’s recollection of her dream to characterize Satan’s unrequited yearning for Heaven in the typical style of Petrarchan love poetry. The dream, although told to us through the voice of Eve, is in fact a metaphysical conjuring by Satan, who is attempting to entice Eve into eating from the Tree of Knowledge. As such, we as readers can consider the occurrences within the dream as Satan’s personal idea of Heaven, and as such, an insight towards Satan’s longing for Heaven.

The dream begins with Satan’s beckoning of Eve from a gentle whisper in her ear. This whisper is not a simple calling of Eve’s name; rather, Satan describes the beauty of Heaven, with the cool night and the sing-song birds. Satan’s description ends with the fully iambic phrase, “now reigns / Full-orbed the moon, and, with more pleasing light” (5:41-42). The iambic meter suggests the fantastical nature of beauty in Satan’s manifestation of Heaven.

Satan’s description of his Heaven compares to the depiction of Petrarch’s Laura which often likens the woman to some supraphysiological beauty (i.e. Sonnet XII “Enthrones in perfect beauty like a star, \ As far as she outshines the rest” (1)). However, Satan comes to the forlorn conclusion that Heaven’s beauty is in fact not for him and tells Eve, “Shadowy sets off the face of things – in vain, / If none regards.” (5:43-44). Milton begins the phrase with stressed anti-iambic, leading right off the perfect iambic meter from line 42. Furthermore, Milton delays the crux of the phrase “– in vain [!]”, to augment the emotion in Satan’s speech. With this meter and phrasing, Milton is indicating Satan’s genuine sorrow in the fact that there is no one to take in Heaven’s beauty. Again, the alternation between the bliss and sorrow of love is akin to the tone common in Petrarchan love poetry. The alternation between extreme emotion of Petrarchan love is especially apparent in Petrarch and Shakespeare poems on unrequited love, as is the case with Satan and his Heaven.

Let us fast-forward in the dream to Eve seeing Satan as the Heavenly angel captivated by the Tree of Knowledge. Satan admires the beauty of the Tree of Knowledge and laments that the taste of its fruit is forbidden (5:58-61). However, Satan then proceeds to break the convention of Petrarchan love by plucking a fruit and tasting it (5:65). Petrarch himself never actualized his love with Laura, and Petrarchan love poems are primarily an account of his thoughts while in love and after her death, but never of their interaction. Similarly, Shakespeare never becomes acquainted with the Dark Lady, who instead associates herself with the Fair Youth. The idea of achieving love is not conducive with the Petrarch scheme of attraction. And to the realization that Satan had attained the source of his longing, Eve is bewildered telling Adam, “Me damp horror chilled \ At such bold words vouched with a deed so bold” (5:65-66). Milton allows for repeat dactyl meters with an added stress on “Me damp horror chilled” (- / / /) (5:65), and “At such bold words” (- / / /) (5:66). The repetition reinforces the oddity of breaking the Petrarchan scheme of unrequited love, where Satan actually realizes his longing for Heaven.

Once he eats the fruit, Satan returns to Eve and talks to her with a perfect, soothing iambic meter, “Sweet of thyself … happy though thou art” (5:68-75). Satan breaks the iambic meter with an anti-iambic only to tell Eve, with emphasis, “Happier thou may’st be, worthier canst not be” (5:76). Fooled by Satan’s poetic speech, in the dream, Eve also consumes the fruit of knowledge and soars into the sky with Satan, only to fall once Satan disappears (5:85-91). As readers, we can read this fall as the metaphoric Fall of Man.

With an understanding of the archetypal Petrarch love, Satan’s conjured dream and Eve’s retelling can be seen as Satan’s nostalgic longing for Heaven. Milton uses the model of Petrarchan love in Satan’s depiction of Heaven only to emphatically break it. Eve recants her horror, conveying to the audience the magnitude of the moment. We are to understand that the disruption of the idealistic Petrarchan love is the beginning of Satan’s connivery. Milton thus uses the archetype of Petrarchan love to push the narrative of Satan’s cunning and his deception towards Eve.
